Digital Currency Exchanges to be Regulated in Australia

From 3 Apr 2018, DCEs come under the AML/CTF Act: registration with AUSTRAC, CDD, reporting, and record‚Äëkeeping.

Read the short extract

From 3 April 2018, Digital Currency Exchange (DCE) service providers became subject to Australia‚Äôs AML/CTF Act following passage of the AML/CTF Amendment Act 2017. This reform addressed growing financial‚Äëcrime risks in the crypto‚Äëasset sector. DCEs must register with AUSTRAC, conduct customer identification and verification, adopt written AML/CTF programs, file suspicious matter reports and threshold transaction reports (cash ‚â• AUD 10,000), and maintain records for seven years. Police clearances are mandated for key personnel. At the time, numerous Australian exchanges (ACX, BTC Markets, CoinJar, CoinTree) and international players serving Australian clients (Coinbase, TransferWise, OFX) prepared compliance frameworks rapidly. For AML officers, this marked a shift from voluntary measures to enforceable obligations, placing Australia among early jurisdictions to regulate digital currencies formally under AML law.

Source: AUSTRAC guidance; AML/CTF Amendment Act 2017; Royal Assent 13 Dec 2017.

On 20 December 2017, the European Parliament and Council reached agreement on the 5th Anti‚ÄëMoney Laundering Directive (5AMLD), a major step in the EU‚Äôs fight against financial crime. 5AMLD requires greater public access to beneficial ownership registers, extends the powers of Financial Intelligence Units, and imposes stricter safeguards for transactions linked to high‚Äërisk countries. For AML compliance officers, 5AMLD highlighted the urgency of updating CDD processes, expanding oversight of VASPs, and monitoring cross‚Äëborder flows linked to high‚Äërisk jurisdictions.

Punjab National Bank Fraud Exposed

PNB discloses >$1.7bn LoU fraud tied to Nirav Modi/Mehul Choksi; serious control gaps in trade finance and SWIFT use.

Read the short extract

On 14 February 2018, Punjab National Bank (PNB) disclosed a massive fraud involving unauthorized letters of undertaking (LoUs), later estimated at over $1.7 billion. Rogue staff allegedly issued LoUs via SWIFT, enabling companies linked to jeweler Nirav Modi and Mehul Choksi to obtain overseas credit. The fraud bypassed PNB‚Äôs core banking system, exposing serious internal control gaps. Indian authorities opened investigations, while international confirming banks faced questions on reliance on PNB guarantees. For AML professionals, the scandal highlights vulnerabilities in trade finance, especially when manual processes circumvent automated monitoring. Programs should stress‚Äëtest LoU controls, ensure segregation of duties in SWIFT operations, and reinforce scrutiny on high‚Äëvalue clients in sectors vulnerable to corruption and fraud.

Source: Reserve Bank of India; PNB filings; Indian press (Feb 2018).

U.S. Bank N.A. Deferred Prosecution Agreement

DOJ DPA; forfeiture $528m; parallel FinCEN/OCC/Fed penalties over alert‚Äëcapping and missed SARs.

Read the short extract

On 15 February 2018, the U.S. Department of Justice announced a deferred prosecution agreement (DPA) with U.S. Bank N.A. for willful BSA/AML violations. The bank admitted capping the number of transaction‚Äëmonitoring alerts its systems could generate, leading to missed suspicious activity reports (SARs). It agreed to forfeit $528 million, while parallel actions imposed penalties of $185m (FinCEN), $75m (OCC), and $15m (Federal Reserve). Regulators highlighted failures to staff compliance adequately and to escalate issues transparently. For AML teams, the case reinforces the risks of resource constraints, poor model governance, and misaligned incentives. Alert thresholds must be based on risk, not convenience, and supervisors must be kept apprised of known gaps.

Source: DOJ press release; FinCEN, OCC, Federal Reserve orders, 15 Feb 2018.

UK Issues First Unexplained Wealth Orders

New investigative tool under the Criminal Finances Act 2017 targets suspected illicit wealth holdings.

On 28 February 2018, UK authorities confirmed the first use of Unexplained Wealth Orders (UWOs), introduced by the Criminal Finances Act 2017. While details of the individuals involved were not disclosed, the National Crime Agency signalled UWOs would focus on politically exposed persons (PEPs) and individuals linked to serious crime. For financial institutions, UWOs highlight regulators‚Äô increasing reliance on asset‚Äëbased measures to disrupt illicit finance.
