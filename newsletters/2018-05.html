The New Fifth Pillar of BSA/AML Compliance

FinCEN‚Äôs CDD Beneficial Ownership Rule adds a fifth pillar: BO identification, risk profiling, and ongoing monitoring.

Read the short extract

For decades, U.S. financial institutions have been expected to maintain four key elements of a BSA/AML compliance program: (1) internal policies and controls, (2) a designated compliance officer, (3) ongoing training, and (4) independent review. In 2016, FinCEN‚Äôs Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Final Rule introduced a fifth pillar that came fully into effect on 11 May 2018. Covered institutions‚Äîincluding banks, broker-dealers, mutual funds, futures commission merchants, and introducing brokers‚Äîmust now identify and verify the beneficial owners of legal-entity customers. They must also understand the nature and purpose of customer relationships, develop risk profiles, and conduct ongoing monitoring to update information and file SARs as appropriate. The rule was designed to close gaps around shell and anonymous companies, and to standardize practices that had been applied unevenly. For AMLCOs, the fifth pillar codified customer-risk understanding as central to program effectiveness.

Source: FinCEN Customer Due Diligence Final Rule; compliance deadline 11 May 2018.

APRA Criticises Commonwealth Bank of Australia (CBA)

APRA‚Äôs prudential inquiry cites cultural and governance failings; CBA to hold extra AUD 1bn capital.

Read the short extract

On 1 May 2018, the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) released a scathing report on the Commonwealth Bank of Australia (CBA), highlighting deep cultural and governance failings. The 110-page report found that CBA‚Äôs ‚Äúcontinued financial success dulled the senses of the institution‚Äù and created a complacent, insular culture. Treasurer Scott Morrison described the report as ‚Äúrequired reading‚Äù for all Australian financial institutions. Story 2

EU Parliament Approves 5AMLD

Parliament backs stronger BO access, prepaid thresholds, crypto scope, FIU cooperation, and account registries.

Read the short extract

On 19 April 2018, the European Parliament voted to adopt the Fifth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (5AMLD), a landmark package strengthening the EU‚Äôs AML framework. 5AMLD requires member states to establish centralised bank and payment account registries, expand public access to beneficial ownership registers, and extend AML obligations to virtual currency exchanges and custodian wallet providers. It also lowers identification thresholds for prepaid cards, strengthens enhanced due diligence on high-risk countries, and improves cross-border FIU cooperation. The directive was a direct response to the Panama Papers and other financial crime scandals, reflecting growing EU determination to tackle opacity in financial flows. For AMLCOs, 5AMLD set a clear timetable for significant policy and procedural updates across Europe, particularly in onboarding, BO verification, and crypto-asset monitoring.

Source: European Parliament press release; 5AMLD vote, 19 Apr 2018.

FinCEN Issues FAQs on Beneficial Ownership/CDD Rule

FAQs (FIN‚Äë2018‚ÄëG001) clarify 25% ownership + control prong, updating BO data, and risk-based application.

Read the short extract

On 3 April 2018, FinCEN published detailed FAQs (FIN-2018-G001) to clarify expectations under the Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Beneficial Ownership Rule, which became enforceable on 11 May 2018. The FAQs addressed key operational questions: how to apply the 25% ownership test plus one control prong; treatment of multiple accounts for the same customer; interaction with existing risk-based CDD practices; and updating customer information when risk profiles change. FinCEN stressed that the rule codifies practices many institutions already followed but now sets a binding baseline. For AML professionals, the guidance provided practical direction to finalise policies, systems, and training in advance of the compliance deadline. It also reinforced expectations that BO data should be actively maintained and leveraged in suspicious activity monitoring.

Source: FinCEN Guidance FIN‚Äë2018‚ÄëG001, 3 Apr 2018.

Australia Brings Digital Currency Exchanges into AML Regime

From 3 Apr, DCEs must register with AUSTRAC, perform KYC, report SMRs/threshold cash, and keep records.

Read the short extract

On 3 April 2018, AUSTRAC announced that Digital Currency Exchanges (DCEs) are now formally regulated under the AML/CTF Act. From this date, DCEs operating in Australia must register with AUSTRAC, conduct customer identification and verification (KYC), monitor and report suspicious matters, file threshold transaction reports for cash ‚â• AUD 10,000, and maintain records. Registration requires key personnel to pass suitability checks, and non-compliant operators face civil and criminal penalties. The reform made Australia one of the first jurisdictions to explicitly bring virtual currency providers into its AML regime. For AML officers, the change underscored the need to identify counterparties with crypto exposure, assess whether they are AUSTRAC-registered, and update due diligence policies to reflect new obligations. The move also foreshadowed FATF‚Äôs 2019 global standards for virtual assets.

Source: AUSTRAC announcement; AML/CTF Amendment Act commencement, 3 Apr 2018.
