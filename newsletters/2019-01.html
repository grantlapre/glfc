GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 2, Issue 1 — January 2019

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 31 December 2018.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EU gears up for 5AMLD implementation

Member States progressed national transposition plans for the Fifth Anti‑Money Laundering Directive, expanding scope to virtual‑asset service providers, prepaid instruments, and enhanced access to beneficial‑ownership registers.

By year‑end 2018, many institutions had launched change programs to close gaps for crypto‑exposed onboarding, strengthen source‑of‑funds checks, and connect workflows to company‑ownership registers. Groups operating across multiple EU markets prioritised consistency in KYC standards and clarified responsibilities for collecting, verifying, and refreshing ownership data ahead of supervisory reviews.

Beneficial‑ownership transparency tightens in Europe

Several EU jurisdictions advanced public or semi‑public company‑ownership databases under 4AMLD/5AMLD requirements.

Compliance teams prepared to leverage registries as a primary data source, reconciling discrepancies between client attestations and official records. Firms also planned guardrails for data‑quality issues, creating audit trails that show how registry checks informed risk ratings, periodic reviews, and escalation decisions.

U.S. CDD Rule bedding‑in after 2018 go‑live

Following the May 2018 effective date, U.S. institutions refined beneficial‑ownership collection and exception management as regulators signalled expectations for consistent execution.

Program owners focused on control effectiveness over checklists—standardising documentary evidence across channels, tightening validation of ownership thresholds, and upgrading case notes so investigators can trace how ownership structures affect alert disposition and SAR narratives.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

Ongoing focus on Iran and Pakistan under FATF monitoring

Risk‑based controls remained elevated for corridors involving jurisdictions under FATF monitoring, with continued attention to TF vulnerabilities.

Banks re‑evaluated correspondent exposure, ensured list‑ingestion processes were timely, and strengthened enhanced due diligence for customers with links to higher‑risk regions. Training refreshed investigative cues so analysts could recognise cross‑border TF indicators embedded in routine payments.

UN sanctions updates require rapid screening hygiene

Late‑2018 UN listings and amendments related to ISIL/Al‑Qaida networks required immediate screening updates and match‑management discipline.

Institutions stress‑tested alert‑pause procedures, streamlined escalation paths, and improved documentation standards to show clear reasoning from alert to decision—supporting both audit and supervisory scrutiny.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Large‑scale data breaches heighten fraud exposure

Major breach disclosures at the end of 2018 underscored persistent risks around account takeover, synthetic identities, and card‑not‑present fraud.

Fraud teams coordinated with cyber colleagues to translate indicators of compromise into payment‑risk controls: velocity limits by merchant and BIN, device/IP analytics, and tighter step‑up authentication for unusual patterns. Issuers readied targeted re‑issuance campaigns and customer education to dampen downstream losses.

Nordic/Baltic investigations continue to reshape risk appetite

European authorities pressed on with probes into historic cross‑border flows through Baltic branches of Nordic banks.

Boards across the region strengthened affiliate oversight, refreshed risk assessments for non‑resident and high‑risk customer segments, and increased independent testing. The situation reinforced how quickly correspondent reputations can be affected by weak controls at subsidiaries and partners.

Payments ecosystem: de‑risking and fintech oversight

Supervisors examined agent/program‑manager arrangements and safeguarding practices among fast‑growing payment institutions.

Banks and PSPs re‑checked governance around outsourced KYC, reconciled funds‑safeguarding accounts, and ensured that rapid merchant onboarding did not outpace AML, sanctions, and fraud controls—especially for cross‑border e‑commerce and high‑risk MCCs.
