GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 3, Issue 2 — February 2020

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 31 January 2020.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EU 5AMLD Implementation Deadline (10 Jan 2020)

EU Member States were required to transpose the Fifth Anti‑Money Laundering Directive, extending AML coverage to virtual‑asset service providers and prepaid instruments.

The milestone marked a step change in regulatory expectations for crypto exchanges, custodial wallets, and prepaid cards. Institutions had to update KYC procedures, adapt transaction monitoring to detect crypto typologies, and ensure beneficial‑ownership registers were accessible. Supervisors signalled they would scrutinise late transposition closely, raising compliance risks for lagging jurisdictions.

UK FCA ‘Dear CEO’ Letter on AML Controls (January 2020)

The FCA issued a sector‑wide letter warning firms to address weaknesses in governance, customer due diligence, and transaction monitoring.

The letter underscored that AML failings would not be tolerated under the Senior Managers Regime. Boards were expected to evidence clear accountability, resource investment, and remediation timelines. Firms reviewed monitoring backlogs, enhanced EDD files, and documented governance reporting channels to avoid supervisory censure.

U.S. FinCEN Beneficial Ownership Enforcement

FinCEN reiterated its focus on enforcing the Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Rule and beneficial‑ownership collection.

Institutions refreshed their CDD policies, ensuring collection processes were consistent across onboarding channels. Compliance teams also re‑evaluated exception management, enhanced data validation controls, and trained staff on scenarios where beneficial ownership information could be incomplete or misleading.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

UN Sanctions Updates (January 2020)

The UN issued new listings against ISIL/Al‑Qaida affiliates, requiring financial institutions to update screening systems promptly.

These listings reinforced the importance of daily sanctions‑list ingestion and system reconciliation. Institutions rehearsed escalation playbooks, tested system coverage across payment channels, and documented how sanctions updates were communicated enterprise‑wide.

FATF Monitoring Context Ahead of February Plenary

Ahead of its February meeting, FATF signalled continuing pressure on jurisdictions including Iran and Pakistan, with potential implications for monitoring obligations.

Banks reviewed their correspondent exposure to higher‑risk jurisdictions and prepared for possible list adjustments. Risk teams refreshed customer risk ratings and ensured that sanctions and AML monitoring rules aligned with FATF’s evolving expectations.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Early COVID‑19 Fraud Warnings

Authorities in Asia and Europe reported scams involving counterfeit medical goods and phishing campaigns exploiting coronavirus fears.

Though still in early stages, these schemes foreshadowed how criminals adapt rapidly to global crises. Banks tightened monitoring for unusual cross‑border trade in medical goods, warned customers about phishing, and added typologies for suspicious charity fundraising.

Danske Bank and Nordic Investigations Continue

Regulators maintained pressure on Danske Bank and peers linked to Baltic money‑laundering scandals, with enforcement discussions ongoing in early 2020.

The persistence of Nordic probes showed the long tail of legacy AML failures. Boards across Europe reassessed oversight of high‑risk affiliates, ensured escalation frameworks were functioning, and increased investment in transaction‑monitoring and compliance culture reforms.
