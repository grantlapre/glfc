GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 2, Issue 12 — December 2019

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 30 November 2019.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EU 5AMLD — final preparations ahead of January 2020 deadline

Member States and institutions rushed to finalise compliance with the Fifth AML Directive, expanding coverage to virtual‑asset service providers, prepaid instruments, and beneficial‑ownership transparency.

By late 2019, firms accelerated onboarding changes for crypto exchanges and custodial wallets, reviewed prepaid card programs, and ensured access to ownership registers. Supervisors warned of strict enforcement from January, prompting many groups to fast‑track residual remediation projects.

6AMLD planning — liability and predicate offences

Institutions continued gap‑analyses for 6AMLD’s wider predicate offences, aiding/abetting liability and harmonised penalties due in 2020.

Investigations, legal, and HR codified playbooks for case handling, clarified escalation thresholds, and embedded record‑keeping standards. Training plans highlighted serious‑crime typologies and evidentiary discipline to ensure readiness for prosecutorial expectations.

FATF follow‑up monitoring

Ongoing FATF follow‑up processes sustained pressure on jurisdictions with AML/CFT deficiencies, keeping some countries on increased monitoring lists.

Banks refreshed country‑risk ratings, tuned transaction‑monitoring rules for higher‑risk corridors, and re‑evaluated correspondent exposure. Governance reporting began emphasising effectiveness measures—quality SARs and interdictions—over volume metrics.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

Sanctions maintenance across UN/EU/US regimes

November brought routine terrorism‑related amendments requiring near‑real‑time screening hygiene and disciplined match management.

Institutions validated ingestion across screening engines, rehearsed pause‑and‑review steps, and improved documentation to ensure audit‑ready alert decisions while preserving efficiency for clean traffic.

Focus on small‑value flows and online fundraising

Authorities reiterated the risks of micro‑donations, informal value transfer, and online platforms linked to higher‑risk corridors.

Monitoring scenarios were adapted to detect aggregation across linked accounts, while analysts were coached to capture contextual details—geography, beneficiary links, and transaction purpose—in SARs for FIUs.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Holiday‑season fraud pressures

Card‑not‑present fraud, phishing, and mule activity spiked during peak retail periods, with criminals exploiting consumer urgency.

Banks layered device fingerprinting and behavioural analytics with transaction velocity limits, enforced call‑back verification for beneficiary changes, and launched customer‑education campaigns. Mule‑account detection was reinforced with device/IP analytics and peer information‑sharing.

Ransomware and BEC remain persistent

Corporates and municipalities continued to report ransomware and business‑email compromise disrupting financial workflows.

AML/fraud teams partnered with cyber to translate indicators (domains, wallets, IPs) into monitoring rules, enriched SAR narratives with blockchain‑analytics context, and recorded remediation gaps to support potential recovery or regulatory action.

Emerging deepfake‑enabled fraud

Incidents of synthetic audio and video manipulation raised new concerns for identity verification and payment authorisation controls.

Programs piloted biometric and behavioural verification for high‑risk transactions, introduced multi‑person approvals, and coached staff on recognising urgency cues and unusual phrasing in potentially manipulated communications.

DATA CORNER

  • EU: 5AMLD compliance projects accelerated ahead of Jan 2020 enforcement.
  • EU: 6AMLD liability/predicate‑offence gap‑closure continued.
  • Fraud: holiday‑season CNP fraud, ransomware, and deepfake risks elevated.

LOOKING AHEAD

  • EU: 5AMLD formally effective from 10 January 2020 — strict supervisory stance expected.
  • EU: 6AMLD transposition due in 2020 — complete gap remediation and training.
  • Fraud: prepare for evolving synthetic‑media threats and continuing mule recruitment.
