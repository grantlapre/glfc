AML Risks Associated with Gold

Gold‚Äôs liquidity and value‚Äëdensity make it a perennial laundering vehicle; supply‚Äëchain controls are essential.

The global gold trade remains highly attractive to money launderers because of its liquidity, high value‚Äëto‚Äëweight ratio, and ease of cross‚Äëborder movement. Criminal groups exploit the gold sector at multiple stages: theft, purchase of unrefined ore, investment in mines, smelting, refining, and wholesale resale. A common typology involves criminals using illicit cash to purchase refined gold, which is then sold through shell or front companies‚Äîoften at discounted prices‚Äîto disguise origins. The proceeds re‚Äëenter the financial system appearing legitimate. Cash‚Äëintensive wholesalers further complicate oversight by mingling illicit flows with lawful sales. Regulators are increasingly aware: in Australia, proposed ‚ÄúTranche Two‚Äù AML/CTF reforms would bring high value dealers (jewelers, precious‚Äëstone traders, real estate, art dealers) under AUSTRAC obligations. These businesses would need to perform CDD, report suspicious transactions, and maintain compliance programs to close long‚Äëstanding regulatory gaps.

Source: AML Newsletter ‚Äî Insight Article, Feb 2018 edition.

Miami Gold Dealers Sentenced in $3.6 Billion Scheme

Former NTR Metals executives sentenced for laundering illicit gold flows from Latin America through Miami.

In early 2018, U.S. District Judge Robert Scola sentenced former NTR Metals executives Samer Barrage and Juan Granda for their roles in a massive gold‚Äëbased money‚Äëlaundering conspiracy. Operating out of Doral, Florida, they admitted importing tons of illicitly sourced gold from Latin America between 2012 and 2016, channeling more than $3.6 billion through the U.S. precious‚Äëmetals sector. The scheme laundered proceeds of drug trafficking and organized crime, exploiting weak due diligence in the gold supply chain. Barrage received nearly seven years in prison, while Granda was sentenced to six years, reduced for cooperation. Prosecutors credited both for assisting in ongoing investigations and partially repaying illicit profits. The case highlighted systemic vulnerabilities in U.S. precious‚Äëmetals imports, where shell suppliers and falsified paperwork obscured the true origin of gold. For AML officers, it underscored the need for enhanced supply‚Äëchain scrutiny and tighter trade‚Äëfinance controls.

Source: U.S. Department of Justice press release; court filings, Feb 2018.

On 19 January 2018, the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) published its updated public statement on high‚Äërisk and monitored jurisdictions. For compliance teams, the announcement triggered immediate updates to country‚Äërisk matrices, sanctions‚Äëscreening systems, and due‚Äëdiligence frameworks.
