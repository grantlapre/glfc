Security Policy for IT

Clear, enforced IT security policy underpins AML system reliability, data protection and incident response.

Read the short extract

As financial institutions digitize more services, a comprehensive IT security policy has become essential to protecting systems and data against cybercrime. A strong policy should define responsibilities across business units and embed safeguards into daily operations. Core principles include the CIA triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability), supported by controls such as separation of duties, job rotation, incident response planning, forensic testing, and regular training. Organizations must also develop specific standards on incident handling, acceptable use of email, and business continuity/disaster recovery (BCP/DR). Stakeholder involvement‚Äîfrom HR, Legal, and Management to external vendors and regulators‚Äîensures coverage of operational and compliance risks. For AML compliance officers, IT security policy is not a separate silo: it directly impacts monitoring system reliability, SAR reporting timeliness, and customer data protection. Institutions that document, test, and enforce these policies demonstrate governance maturity and resilience against financial crime threats.

Source: AML Newsletter ‚Äî Insight Article, Apr 2018 edition.

Data Breaches Strike CareFirst & Under Armour

Two high‚Äëprofile breaches and a 911 ransomware incident spotlight AML‚Äërelevant cyber risk.

Read the short extract

In April 2018, two major data breaches underscored the rising scale of cyber risk. CareFirst BlueCross BlueShield (Maryland) confirmed that a phishing attack exposed personal data of approximately 6,800 members, including names, identification numbers, and dates of birth; a limited number of Social Security numbers were also compromised. Meanwhile, Under Armour revealed its popular MyFitnessPal app had been hacked, affecting an estimated 150 million users. Compromised data included usernames, hashed passwords, and email addresses. In the same week, the City of Baltimore was hit by a ransomware attack that temporarily disabled its 911 dispatch system, highlighting risks to public infrastructure. For AML compliance officers, these incidents reinforce the importance of cybersecurity in protecting customer data and ensuring continuity of monitoring systems. Breaches can expose institutions to identity theft, account‚Äëtakeover, and fraud typologies‚Äîall of which drive downstream AML risks.

Source: Company disclosures; press reports, Apr 2018.

Story 3

RBI Scraps LoUs/LoCs After PNB Fraud

Trade‚Äëcredit LoUs/LoCs discontinued; banks must rely on LCs/guarantees and tighten controls.

Read the short extract

On 13 March 2018, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) announced the immediate discontinuation of Letters of Undertaking (LoUs) and Letters of Comfort (LoCs) as instruments for trade credit. The decision followed the discovery of the Punjab National Bank (PNB) fraud, where rogue staff issued unauthorized LoUs through SWIFT, enabling overseas borrowing that eventually cost the bank over $1.7 billion. RBI directed that trade credit should henceforth rely only on Letters of Credit (LCs) and bank guarantees, which provide stronger checks. For compliance teams, this was a clear signal to tighten oversight of trade‚Äëfinance processes, especially SWIFT integration, system reconciliations, and manual workarounds. Institutions exposed to Indian trade flows were reminded to revisit risk assessments, monitor correspondent relationships, and strengthen documentary credit controls.

Source: Reserve Bank of India circular, 13 Mar 2018.

TAX3‚Äôs remit included beneficial ownership registers, whistleblower protections, and stronger supervision of financial institutions. For AML professionals, the creation of TAX3 signaled a higher political commitment within the EU to integrate tax transparency and AML agendas. The committee‚Äôs findings were expected to influence the implementation of 5AMLD and shape subsequent EU legislative packages. Institutions were advised to monitor TAX3‚Äôs proceedings for emerging compliance expectations on beneficial ownership, tax‚Äëlinked risks, and cross‚Äëborder reporting obligations.

Source: European Parliament resolution, 1 Mar 2018.
