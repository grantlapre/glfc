GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 3, Issue 1 — January 2020

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 31 December 2019.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EU 5AMLD Transposition Deadline Approaches

Member States were required to transpose the Fifth Anti‑Money Laundering Directive by 10 January 2020, expanding coverage to virtual‑asset service providers and strengthening beneficial‑ownership transparency.

In late 2019, firms accelerated compliance projects to meet the January deadline. Institutions focused on updating onboarding processes for crypto exchanges and custodial wallets, ensuring national beneficial‑ownership registers were accessible, and reviewing prepaid‑card controls. Supervisors signalled they would take a strict stance against delays, raising compliance urgency across the bloc.

Singapore Payment Services Act (effective 28 Jan 2020)

The Monetary Authority of Singapore implemented the PSA, bringing crypto exchanges, wallets, and payment providers under a licensing regime with AML/CTF obligations.

The PSA represented one of the world’s most comprehensive frameworks for regulating digital‑asset intermediaries. For compliance teams, it meant implementing rigorous KYC, reporting, and safeguarding arrangements. Multinationals servicing Asia‑Pacific clients re‑evaluated cross‑border flows and licensing requirements in Singapore, anticipating supervisory scrutiny and opportunities for regulatory arbitrage.

Dutch UBO Register Implementation (2020)

The Netherlands advanced its rollout of an Ultimate Beneficial Ownership register in line with EU directives.

Institutions prepared for a shift toward greater transparency in corporate structures. Compliance teams began integrating registry look‑ups into onboarding and periodic reviews, highlighting discrepancies between self‑attestations and official data. The initiative reinforced the trend toward centralised ownership registries as a cornerstone of effective AML regimes.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

FATF Grey List Updates (Oct 2019 context into Jan 2020)

Pakistan remained under increased monitoring, while other jurisdictions faced scrutiny for deficiencies in counter‑terrorist financing measures.

These outcomes carried over into early 2020, requiring firms to sustain enhanced due diligence across correspondent banking relationships and trade‑finance corridors. Banks also had to adjust risk scoring for customers with exposure to higher‑risk countries, ensuring sanctions and AML monitoring captured terrorism‑financing indicators effectively.

UN Sanctions Designations (late 2019)

The UN Security Council added new ISIL and Al‑Qaida affiliates to its sanctions lists, requiring institutions to update screening tools promptly.

The additions highlighted the need for robust sanctions governance and rapid technology integration. Compliance teams tested list‑update procedures, ensured front‑line staff were trained on escalation protocols, and documented audit trails for supervisory review.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Westpac and AUSTRAC — enforcement proceedings

In late 2019, AUSTRAC filed proceedings against Westpac for AML/CTF breaches, including failures related to child‑exploitation typologies.

The case shocked the industry by its scale, with billions of transactions implicated. It underscored board‑level accountability and forced banks globally to review their detection scenarios for child‑exploitation and cross‑border remittances. The litigation set the stage for record penalties and signalled a tougher enforcement era in Australia.

Nordic and Baltic Bank Investigations Continue

Authorities in Denmark, Sweden, and the Baltics continued investigations into large‑scale money‑laundering flows through Nordic banks’ Baltic branches.

The persistence of these probes highlighted the reputational and financial risks of weak affiliate oversight. Boards across Europe prioritised independent audits of high‑risk branches, strengthened whistleblower channels, and invested in transaction‑monitoring upgrades to mitigate systemic risk.

Global Enforcement Fines 2019 — Year in Review

Industry analyses showed that AML‑related fines worldwide exceeded USD 8 billion in 2019, reflecting rising supervisory assertiveness.

The figures provided boards with a stark benchmark for potential financial exposure. Compliance leaders used the data to reinforce investment cases for technology upgrades, staffing, and governance reforms, stressing that penalties increasingly targeted effectiveness gaps rather than technical breaches alone.
