GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 3, Issue 12 — December 2020

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 30 November 2020.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EU 6AMLD — last‑mile readiness

With the 6th Anti‑Money Laundering Directive transposition deadline in early December, institutions finalised gap‑closing for the expanded list of predicate offences, aiding/abetting liability, and tougher penalties.

November was a final sprint for legal and compliance teams to align policies, case‑handling playbooks, and record‑keeping with 6AMLD’s scope. Groups harmonised approaches across EU subsidiaries, clarified roles for corporate criminal liability, and refreshed training for investigations staff who would face higher thresholds for escalation and documentation.

Beneficial‑ownership transparency momentum

EU and U.S. policy debates continued to converge on stronger company‑ownership transparency and effectiveness‑focused AML programs.

Institutions anticipated that central registries and clearer national priorities would reshape KYC workflows. Compliance leaders prepared to integrate registry data where available, reconcile discrepancies against customer attestations, and evidence how verified ownership information improves risk assessments and SAR narrative quality.

Supervisory focus on program effectiveness

Supervisors across multiple jurisdictions emphasised outcomes—quality of investigations, useful SARs, and measurable disruption of illicit networks—over process volume.

Boards asked for clearer MI on alert quality, tuning, and case outcomes. Functions responded by tightening model‑risk governance for monitoring systems, defining effectiveness KPIs beyond counts, and strengthening feedback loops with FIUs and law enforcement.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

UN / OFAC sanctions activity

November saw continued listings and amendments relating to ISIL/Al‑Qaida affiliates and regional networks, requiring prompt screening updates.

The sustained cadence underscored the need for automated list ingestion and reconciled screening tools to avoid latency. Front‑line teams rehearsed pause‑and‑review procedures and sharpened documentation of alert disposition to withstand audit and supervisory scrutiny.

Heightened attention to small‑value flows and virtual assets

Authorities reiterated that TF networks leverage micro‑donations, informal value transfer, and virtual‑asset rails to circumvent traditional controls.

Firms expanded surveillance logic to aggregate small payments across linked accounts and wallets, applied EDD to VASP relationships, and incorporated blockchain‑analytics indicators into SAR narratives to preserve investigative value.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Pandemic fraud pivots to vaccines and logistics

As vaccine approvals neared, authorities flagged phishing, pre‑order scams, and counterfeit vaccine offers alongside ongoing PPE and relief‑fund fraud.

Criminals adapted quickly to new demand signals, standing up professional‑looking websites and layered mule networks to capture deposits and launder proceeds. Banks refreshed monitoring scenarios around healthcare suppliers and logistics, tightened controls for newly onboarded counterparties, and expanded customer education to blunt social‑engineering attacks.

Ransomware and BEC remain elevated

Financial institutions reported persistent ransomware targeting remote infrastructure and continued business‑email compromise against payment workflows.

Programs integrated IT security telemetry (EDR alerts, anomalous logins) into financial‑crime monitoring so potential account takeovers could be intercepted before funds moved. Payment operations implemented call‑back verification for change‑of‑details requests and imposed cooling‑off periods on high‑risk beneficiary changes.

Cross‑border laundering networks under scrutiny

International investigations into professional money‑laundering networks progressed, building on arrests and seizures announced earlier in the autumn.

Institutions used the enforcement tempo to stress‑test correspondent banking due diligence, refresh typology libraries on cash‑intensive businesses and money mules, and coordinate with peers through information‑sharing partnerships.

DATA CORNER

  • Ongoing: UN/OFAC terrorism‑related designations required continuous screening updates.

  • U.S./EU: Further steps toward beneficial‑ownership transparency and effectiveness metrics.
  • Operational focus: Integrating cyber telemetry with AML/fraud monitoring for faster interdiction.
