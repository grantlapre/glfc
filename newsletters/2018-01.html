Cryptocurrencies ‚Äî A New Frontier in AML Risk

By early 2018, crypto‚Äëassets had gone mainstream‚Äîbringing novel AML/CFT risks that demand enhanced controls.

By early 2018, cryptocurrencies had surged in popularity as speculative assets and alternative payment tools, attracting investors worldwide. Their appeal rests on decentralised blockchain technology, which enables near‚Äëinstant transfers through digital wallets and public/private keys. However, regulators quickly flagged serious financial‚Äëcrime risks: pseudonymity allows criminals to obscure identity, while exchanges often lacked robust KYC/AML controls. Investigations revealed cases where traders were charged for failing to verify customers, highlighting gaps in regulatory frameworks. Law enforcement noted the challenge of following transactions that exist only on distributed ledgers without counterparties clearly identified. For AML officers, the lesson is clear: virtual‚Äëasset exposure demands enhanced due diligence, tighter onboarding protocols, and updated monitoring typologies. With regulators signalling stronger oversight, institutions must treat cryptocurrencies as high‚Äërisk instruments and prepare for integration of crypto‚Äëasset service providers into formal AML regimes.

AUSTRAC Expands Civil Penalty Case Against CBA

Dec 2017: Regulator adds allegations that push contraventions beyond 53,800; increased focus on IDMs and monitoring.

On 14 December 2017, AUSTRAC announced a major escalation of its enforcement against the Commonwealth Bank of Australia (CBA). The regulator added 100 further alleged contraventions to its civil penalty proceedings, bringing the total to more than 53,800 breaches of the AML/CTF Act. Allegations included CBA‚Äôs failure to submit two suspicious matter reports related to terrorism financing within 24 hours, and delays or omissions in reporting a further 54 suspicious matters tied to law‚Äëenforcement investigations. AUSTRAC also identified weak monitoring of accounts even after clear structuring and terrorism‚Äëfinancing indicators were observed, particularly around CBA‚Äôs Intelligent Deposit Machines (IDMs). Each breach carried a maximum penalty of $21 million, placing the bank at significant financial and reputational risk. For AML teams, the case highlighted the importance of timely reporting, operational controls, and regulator collaboration through initiatives such as the Fintel Alliance.

Source: AUSTRAC press release and court filings, 14 Dec 2017.

UK Sanctions & Anti‚ÄëMoney Laundering Bill Progresses

Dec 2017: SAMLA advances through Lords Committee, paving a post‚ÄëBrexit framework.

In December 2017, the UK Parliament advanced the Sanctions and Anti‚ÄëMoney Laundering Bill (SAMLA) through House of Lords Committee stages, part of preparations for a post‚ÄëBrexit framework.
