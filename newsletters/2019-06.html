GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 2, Issue 6 — June 2019

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 31 May 2019.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

Virtual‑asset supervision — global guidance imminent

Supervisors signalled that detailed expectations for exchanges, custodial wallets, and other VASPs would be formalised imminently, with emphasis on licensing, CDD, travel‑rule compliance, and ongoing monitoring.

Firms inventoried exposure to crypto‑native counterparties, set EDD triggers for higher‑risk patterns, and prepared to implement data‑sharing mechanisms for originator/beneficiary information. Correspondent and payment‑rail relationships were reviewed to prevent risk obfuscation via processors and nested flows.

EU 5AMLD — enforcement posture strengthens

Member States moved from transposition to active supervision, with a particular focus on beneficial‑ownership register access and usage by obliged entities.

Groups aligned KYC to the strictest national baseline, embedded registry look‑ups into onboarding and periodic reviews, and documented how ownership data changed risk ratings, EDD scope, and SAR narratives. Exception handling was tightened where registers were incomplete or conflicted with other sources.

6AMLD preparation — predicate‑offence and liability mapping

Institutions advanced gap‑analysis for 6AMLD’s expanded predicate offences, aiding/abetting and attempt provisions, and harmonised penalties ahead of 2020 transposition.

Compliance, investigations, and legal teams aligned on case‑handling playbooks and record‑keeping standards. Training prioritised serious‑crime typologies and evidentiary discipline so files could withstand prosecutorial scrutiny.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

Sanctions maintenance across UN/EU/US regimes

Routine listings and amendments in May targeted ISIL/Al‑Qaida affiliates and facilitators, requiring rapid screening updates and disciplined match management.

Institutions tightened daily ingestion cadences and validated consistent propagation of list changes across customer, payment, and trade‑finance screening. First‑line teams rehearsed pause‑and‑review procedures and ensured audit‑ready documentation for alert decisions.

NPO flows and small‑value payment vigilance

Authorities reiterated the TF risks of small, recurring donations routed via online platforms and informal value‑transfer networks.

Monitoring scenarios were tuned to detect aggregation across linked accounts and channels; analysts were trained to recognise narratives indicative of misuse. SARs emphasised context—beneficiary links, geography, and purpose—to preserve investigative value for FIUs.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Ransomware and cyber‑enabled fraud surge

Hospitals, municipalities, and enterprises reported disruptive ransomware incidents, with criminals seeking faster payouts via crypto and money‑mule networks.

Fraud and AML teams coordinated with cyber to translate indicators (suspicious domains, wallets, IPs) into controls. Banks enhanced SAR narratives with blockchain‑analytics context, implemented cooling‑off periods for first‑time beneficiaries, and reinforced customer education to reduce social‑engineering success.

SIM‑swap and account‑takeover techniques evolve

Telecom port‑out fraud and OTP interception drove spikes in payment fraud and unauthorised access.

Institutions layered device fingerprinting, behavioural biometrics, and out‑of‑band verification for sensitive changes. Payment operations enforced call‑backs for beneficiary edits and monitored velocity on first‑time payments to new payees.

Money‑mule recruitment intensifies

Seasonal recruitment of students and vulnerable customers increased mule activity used to cash‑out cyber and scam proceeds.

Banks implemented targeted education and alerts, tightened controls for high‑risk incoming credits, and tuned monitoring for rapid pass‑through patterns—coordinating with industry sharing groups to disrupt networks.

DATA CORNER

  • Virtual assets: detailed supervisory expectations imminent (licensing, CDD, travel‑rule).
  • EU: 5AMLD enforcement posture strengthening; UBO registry use under scrutiny.
  • Fraud: Ransomware, SIM‑swap ATO, and mule recruitment elevated.

LOOKING AHEAD

  • Global: Finalised virtual‑asset guidance expected mid‑2019; begin implementation planning.
  • EU: Continue 6AMLD gap‑closure and training on serious‑crime typologies.
  • Fraud: Prepare for summer travel patterns — adjust rules for location anomalies and first‑time payees.
