GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 2, Issue 9 — September 2019

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 31 August 2019.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EBA report highlights EU‑wide AML/CFT risk weaknesses

Mid‑2019 analysis from European authorities underscored material gaps in group‑wide risk assessment, transaction monitoring, and data quality across several sectors.

Boards were urged to evidence end‑to‑end ownership of financial‑crime risks and to allocate resources proportionate to exposure. Programs translated findings into concrete actions: refreshing enterprise risk assessments, tightening model‑risk governance for monitoring systems, and enhancing data lineage so investigators can trust alerts and reconstruct decisions for audit and supervisors.

Virtual‑asset supervision — national frameworks take shape

Following FATF’s June guidance, jurisdictions began sketching licensing/registration regimes for exchanges and custodial wallets, with emphasis on CDD, suspicious reporting and the travel‑rule.

Banks inventoried relationships with crypto‑native firms and payment processors, set EDD triggers for high‑risk patterns, and piloted solutions to exchange originator/beneficiary data between VASPs. Correspondent and card‑acquiring partners reviewed controls to prevent risk transfer through nested or obfuscated flows.

Beneficial‑ownership registers — operationalisation focus

Member States progressed access to company‑ownership databases under 4AMLD/5AMLD, with supervisors asking firms to demonstrate actual use in onboarding and periodic reviews.

Compliance teams embedded registry look‑ups into workflow tools, reconciled records against client attestations, and documented how discrepancies drove EDD, relationship decisions, and SAR narratives. Exception‑handling and evidence standards were tightened to withstand supervisory challenge.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

Sanctions maintenance across UN/EU/US regimes

August saw routine terrorism‑related list updates across multiple regimes, requiring prompt screening hygiene and consistent alert handling.

Institutions validated near‑real‑time ingestion across customer, payments and trade‑finance screening, rehearsed pause‑and‑review procedures, and strengthened documentation so alert decisions were audit‑ready while keeping clean traffic flowing.

Focus on small‑value flows and online fundraising

Authorities reiterated TF risks tied to micro‑donations, informal transfer systems, and online platforms.

Monitoring scenarios were tuned to detect aggregation across linked accounts and channels; analysts trained to spot narratives and counterparties indicative of misuse. SARs highlighted geography, beneficiary links and payment purpose to preserve investigative value for FIUs.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Ransomware and cyber‑enabled fraud continue at pace

Enterprises and municipalities reported disruptive incidents, with proceeds channelled through money‑mule networks and virtual‑asset cash‑out points.

AML/fraud teams integrated cyber indicators—wallets, domains and IPs—into monitoring, enforced cooling‑off periods for first‑time beneficiaries, and strengthened customer education on phishing and remote‑access tool abuse. SAR narratives increasingly incorporated blockchain‑analytics indicators to aid law‑enforcement tracing.

Emerging “deepfake voice” social engineering

Reports surfaced of voice‑cloning used to authorise fraudulent transfers, highlighting vulnerabilities in call‑back and verbal confirmation controls.

Programs revisited out‑of‑band verification and multi‑person approvals for high‑risk changes, deployed biometric and behavioural checks where feasible, and coached finance teams on recognising urgency ploys and unusual language patterns in voice authorisations.

APP scams and mule recruitment

Authorised push‑payment fraud targeted customers via spoofed supplier invoices and romance/investment schemes, feeding expanding mule networks.

Banks tightened beneficiary‑change safeguards, implemented confirmation‑of‑payee and warning frictions, and coordinated with peers to identify mule clusters using device, IP and account‑behaviour analytics. Education campaigns targeted small businesses and vulnerable consumers.
