GLFC AML / CFT / Fraud & Financial Crime Newsletter

Volume 2, Issue 2 — February 2019

This issue captures key developments across Anti‑Money Laundering (AML), Counter‑Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and Fraud & Financial Crime as at 31 January 2019.

AML — Regulatory & Policy Developments

EU beneficial‑ownership registers gain momentum

Member States accelerated work to operationalise company‑ownership databases under 4AMLD/5AMLD, expanding access for obliged entities and authorities.

For cross‑border groups, early 2019 was about wiring registry look‑ups into onboarding and periodic reviews and documenting how mismatches with customer attestations were handled. Programs prioritised data‑quality controls, audit trails for each check, and escalation pathways when registers lacked depth or conflicted with other sources.

Virtual‑asset supervision moves onto regulators’ 2019 agenda

Following FATF’s October 2018 updates on Recommendation 15, supervisors began signalling expectations for AML controls at exchanges, custodial wallets, and related service providers.

Institutions mapped their exposure to crypto‑native businesses and high‑risk counterparties, defining when enhanced due diligence and ongoing monitoring would apply. Banks also examined correspondent and payment‑processor relationships to ensure risk‑transfer from crypto activities was identified and controlled rather than obscured in aggregate flows.

Supervisors emphasise effectiveness over policy volume

Early‑year speeches and notices from several authorities highlighted program outcomes—useful SARs and timely investigations—over mere procedure completeness.

Compliance leaders responded by tuning alert models, refreshing quality‑assurance criteria for investigations, and building dashboards that tracked investigative value rather than only counts. The shift also pushed teams to integrate fraud, cyber, and sanctions signals into cases to surface richer narratives for FIUs.

CFT — Counter‑Terrorism Financing

UN sanctions updates and match‑management discipline

January amendments to ISIL/Al‑Qaida designations required rapid list ingestion and consistent alert handling across screening systems.

Banks rehearsed pause‑and‑review protocols, validated cross‑system synchronisation, and strengthened documentation from first alert to final decision. The goal was to withstand audit scrutiny while keeping legitimate payments flowing.

Preparing for FATF Plenary outcomes

With a February FATF meeting approaching, institutions reviewed exposure to monitored jurisdictions and refreshed TF risk assessments.

Teams stress‑tested wire‑payment rules for higher‑risk corridors, ensured sanctions screening and AML monitoring were aligned, and updated EDD triggers tied to TF red flags such as opaque charitable flows and unusual cash‑intensive activity.

Fraud & Financial Crime — Cases & Trends

Credential dumps drive account‑takeover risk

Large credential compilations disclosed in January elevated the risk of credential‑stuffing and downstream account takeover across financial platforms.

Fraud teams implemented device and IP reputation checks, rate‑limits on login attempts, and step‑up authentication for anomalous behaviour. Banks also coordinated with cyber teams to monitor dark‑web chatter and proactively notify affected customers to reset passwords.

Payments ecosystem oversight tightens

Supervisors scrutinised program‑manager and agent models in fast‑growing payment institutions for weaknesses around onboarding and safeguarding.

Institutions re‑checked outsourced KYC controls, clarified responsibilities in contracts, and ensured transaction monitoring covered sub‑merchant activity. The work aimed to prevent blind spots where high‑risk MCCs or cross‑border e‑commerce could evade detection.

Baltic/Nordic legacy flows remain under the microscope

Regulators and prosecutors continued to examine historic non‑resident flows through Baltic branches, sustaining pressure on regional banks.

Boards strengthened oversight of high‑risk affiliates, mandated independent look‑backs, and expanded whistleblower channels. Correspondent banks demanded clearer evidence of remediation before restoring normal risk appetite.
